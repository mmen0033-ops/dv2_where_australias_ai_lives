Section 3: The cables

How data physically gets in and out of the country

Australia is an island, so every byte going overseas travels through undersea fibre cables. This dataset tracks the fourteen major international cables that carry the bulk of that traffic (a fifteenth runs domestically, linking Sydney and Perth), and every one of the international cables lands at just three cities: Sydney, Perth and Darwin. Sydney alone takes nine of the fourteen. A handful of smaller cables land elsewhere, notably a newer hub on the Sunshine Coast, but the heavy capacity stays concentrated in these three points.

How to read Each line is a submarine cable running from an Australian city to another country. Thicker lines carry more data. Darker lines are newer. Tbps (terabits per second) measures how much data a cable can carry at once; one Tbps is roughly 1,000 home internet connections at full speed.
Mercator projection centred on the Asia-Pacific. Line width shows cable capacity in terabits per second. Darker blue shows newer cables.
Why this matters: Undersea cables carry the international internet between countries. If a cable breaks (from ship anchors, earthquakes, or other damage), traffic has to reroute through the remaining cables. Australia has 15 of them, which sounds like a lot, but they all funnel through the same three landing points.

From the seabed to the server room

Cables don't stop at the beach. After landing, they have to physically connect to a data centre, and most of that bandwidth ends up at the hyperscaler facilities sitting closest to the landing stations.

How to read Blue dots are where cables come ashore. Dark circles are clusters of data centres, sized by total capacity. A grey line connects a landing to any data centre cluster within 50km, showing which clusters sit close enough to use that landing easily.
Cable landings are blue dots. Data centre clusters are dark circles, sized by total MW. Grey lines connect each landing to data centre clusters within 50km.

Section 4: The latency tax

What this means if you live outside Sydney or Melbourne

Concentrating data centres in two cities creates a hidden tax for everyone else, measured in milliseconds. Every kilometre between you and the nearest hyperscaler adds a small amount of delay to every request you make. For loading a webpage you probably won't notice. For real-time AI, online gaming and video calls, you will.

What is latency? The time for a signal to travel to a server and back, measured in milliseconds (thousandths of a second). Light in fibre travels at about two-thirds the speed of light in a vacuum, and real cables wind along trunk routes rather than going in a straight line, so the calculation here multiplies the great-circle distance by a routing factor between 1.4 and 2.5 depending on how far the signal has to travel.
Each shaded area is an ABS SA4 statistical region. Colour shows round-trip latency in milliseconds to the nearest hyperscale data centre. Regions without a measured value in the source dataset are estimated from great-circle distance using the same routing model.

Section 5: Where this leaves us

Where Australia stands, and what is at stake

Australia has built a significant data centre footprint over the past two decades. But that footprint is mostly owned by overseas companies, concentrated in two cities, and connected to the world by just three cable landings. Each of those facts is a choice that could have gone differently.

Cumulative MW installed since 2002. Labels mark when each major hyperscaler opened its first Australian region.
How to read Each row is one cable; its length is the total capacity it carries. Each segment is the share flowing to one nearby operator, coloured by who owns that operator: red for US hyperscalers, amber for other foreign firms, teal for Australian. The more red in a bar, the more of that cable's bandwidth lands with a US-owned operator. Hover any segment for the operator and its exact share, or use the dropdown below the chart to highlight one operator across every cable.
For each cable, the bar shows how its bandwidth divides among nearby operators, weighted by each operator's share of data centre capacity near the landing point.

Australia has the compute. It just doesn't own most of it, and doesn't share it evenly.

The same pattern repeats at every scale, and the numbers are stark. Two states, New South Wales and Victoria, hold roughly 93 percent of national capacity, leaving the other five states and both territories to split what remains. Five operators control 85 percent of the market, and companies based in the United States own around 72 percent of all capacity while Australian firms hold just 20. Every major international cable surfaces at one of three cities, and Sydney alone takes nine of the fourteen. Each of these layers stacks the same way: a small set of places, firms, and chokepoints carries a service the entire country now depends on, so a single fault in the wrong spot, whether a cut cable off Sydney or an outage at one operator, reaches far beyond the city it happens in.

None of this is an accident or a failure of planning. Capacity follows population, fibre, cheap power, and existing networks, so it pools where those already exist. The map simply makes the cost of that logic visible: the regions that host the least are also the ones that wait longest for a response from the nearest hyperscaler, and the parts of the country with the fewest cables are the ones a single break can isolate.
